## Hypothesis

Busy single founders, operators, and ambitious professionals will attend a small activity with
professional and social value more readily than they will adopt another swipe-based dating app.
Trusted context, private intent capture, and structured follow-up will produce mutually confirmed
meetings without a feed or a large open network.

## Beachhead

- Time-poor single founders, operators, and ambitious professional or white-collar workers.
- Members who value ambition, experience, shared context, and a trusted identity signal.
- People willing to attend a useful cowork, coffee, or after-work activity.
- Early testers reachable through the founding team's trusted network.

The launch city is not yet selected. Compare founder and host density, reachable member density,
operating cost, safety coverage, and the team's ability to run recurring events. The San Francisco
Bay Area is a leading network hypothesis, not a committed market.

## Method

- Recruit approximately 20–30 high-quality seed members manually.
- Run a short recurring series, not one isolated event.
- Target roughly 15–25 attendees per event, subject to venue and cohort-quality constraints.
- Begin with cowork, coffee, or after-work formats that retain standalone value.
- Use invite-only distribution and controlled one-person referrals.
- Do not use paid acquisition in the first test.

Candidate formats:

- Saturday Founder Cowork.
- Coffee & Build.
- Founder or Operator Happy Hour.
- Small after-work drinks.
- Profession- or interest-specific cowork/social session.

## Minimum experiment flow

```text
Invited
  → RSVP
  → Attended
  → Private shortlist
  → Mutual interest
  → Follow-up sent
  → Meeting confirmed
  → Meeting completed
  → Second meeting or continued contact
```

The workflow may be operated manually. The experiment tests behavior and outcomes before it tests
software automation.

## Required controls before recruitment

- Select launch city and cohort.
- Define age/eligibility, relationship intent, attendee mix, capacity, host, curation, and referral
  rules.
- Publish code of conduct.
- Assign safety owner, backup, incident channel, removal, and appeal process.
- Freeze public-link, invitation, attendee-list, and mutual-interest privacy.
- Decide whether an invitee must authenticate to view or respond.
- Define meeting-confirmation and cancellation behavior.

## Metrics

### Primary

- Mutually confirmed and completed meetings per activated attendee.

### Funnel

- Invite-to-RSVP conversion.
- RSVP show-up rate.
- Private shortlists per attendee.
- Mutual-interest rate.
- Follow-up sent within seven days.
- Meeting-confirmed and meeting-completed conversion.
- Median event-to-confirmed-meeting time.
- Second meeting or continued contact.

### Retention and distribution

- Repeat attendance.
- Member referral.
- Acquisition source by activated member.

### Guardrails

- Reports, blocks, incidents, removals, appeals, and cohort churn.
- No-show and cancellation.
- Member-reported pressure, ambiguity of romantic intent, or privacy discomfort.
- Operating time and direct cost per event and confirmed meeting.

## Interviews

Ask members:

- Was the activity worthwhile without a romantic match?
- Was romantic intent clear enough without creating pressure?
- Did identity and attendee context create trust?
- Did the shortlist make interest easier or more awkward to express?
- Did follow-up make a meeting more likely than ordinary LinkedIn messages?
- What felt unsafe, exposed, performative, or confusing?
